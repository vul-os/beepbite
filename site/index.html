01 — The problem

Two ways a counter stops belonging to the restaurant

Neither one is a technology problem. Both are ownership problems, and both are what BeepBite is actually for.

CHIT 01

Delivery platforms take the cut and the customer

List on a marketplace app and it takes 15–30% of every order before the kitchen sees a cent — and the platform, not the restaurant, holds the order history, the phone number and the repeat-order relationship. Leave the platform and the customer relationship leaves with it.

BeepBite is not a marketplace. It will not bring a restaurant new customers. What it does is make sure that once someone is a customer, nothing sits between them and the shop the next time they want to order.

CHIT 02

Cloud POS rents you your own till

Cloud point-of-sale vendors charge per terminal, per month, indefinitely — and because the data lives in their database, stopping payment doesn't just stop the bill, it stops the business from reading its own sales history.

BeepBite is a Go API and a React app running against a Postgres database you control. There is no BeepBite service to subscribe to and nobody to cut off — the software is the same whether you paid us anything, because you never do.

No payment facilitator, on purpose

BeepBite records tenders — cash, card, transfer, voucher — it never touches the money itself. "Card" means the restaurant's own card machine on its own counter. No PCI scope, no settlement delay, no cut of revenue taken on the way through.

Tenant scoping isn't a client-side filter

Row-level security is enforced server-side from the authenticated identity, never from a parameter the frontend supplies. Audit log and idempotency keys run throughout, so a retried request can't double-charge a customer or double-count a sale.

02 — Features

Every surface, tagged with its real build state

Front of house, kitchen and stock, money and people, ordering and delivery. Nothing here is claimed as working unless it is — that's the point of the stamps.

№ 01

Touch POS Built

Tabs, splits, voids, comps and manager approval, with a floor plan and table management alongside a customer-facing display.

№ 02

Kitchen display Built

Per-station routing and expo, so an order lands on the right screen in the kitchen without a runner shouting it across the pass.

№ 03

Cash drawer & tenders Built

Drawer sessions and reconciliation across cash, card, transfer and voucher tenders — recorded, never processed.

№ 04

Gift cards & loyalty Built

Gift cards, store credit, house accounts, promotions and coupons, plus invoicing and house-account billing.

№ 05

Inventory & purchasing Built

Recipes and costing, the 86 list, suppliers, purchase orders, goods receipts, invoice matching, waste tracking and reorder suggestions.

№ 06

Delivery & tracking Built

Delivery zones, a driver app and live tracking with a public customer tracking page — built, though less exercised than the POS, and its ETA/map panel has a known bug (see docs).

№ 07

WhatsApp ordering Built

A customer messages the restaurant's number and orders in the app they already use all day. Needs your own Meta Cloud API credentials — off without them.

№ 08

QR-at-table ordering Built

A code on the table opens ordering in the browser, no app install, landing in the same order stream as every other channel.

№ 09

Discord ordering Planned

A channel adapter, same shape as WhatsApp: order in a Discord server a community already uses. Designed, not built.

№ 10

Slack ordering Planned

Order from a Slack workspace — useful for office lunch runs and corporate accounts. Designed, not built.

№ 11

Email ordering Planned

Ordering by email, including over DMTAP, a decentralized mail protocol with no platform operator in the middle. Designed, not built.

№ 12

Every integration is optional Built

WhatsApp, maps and AI are each off unless you supply your own credentials. A fresh install makes no outbound network calls at all.

Ordering is not tied to a single vendor by design: the channel layer is built as pluggable adapters feeding one order stream (see how it works), and WhatsApp being first to ship doesn't make it the identity of the product.

Normalised before the kitchen ever sees it

Every channel adapter normalises into the same order shape before it reaches the kitchen, so orders route to the right station and, if they're going out, to a driver with a tracking link — regardless of whether the order started as a WhatsApp message or a QR scan. Live updates are server-sent events: no polling, no message broker to operate.

The direction is further than any one platform

The dotted adapters above are the direction, not a promise of a date. The furthest end of it is ordering over DMTAP, our own decentralized mail protocol — so that even the sovereign option needs no Meta, no Google, and no platform operator standing between a shop and its customers. Design intent, not shipped code.

04 — Self-hosting

Self-hosting is the only mode

There is no hosted BeepBite, no free tier, no seat count, and no account with us — because there is nothing to sell you. It is MIT/Apache-2.0-licensed software you run on hardware you already have.

Your database, your building

Postgres you control. Nothing phones home, and a fresh install makes no outbound network calls at all. A single-binary-plus-SQLite build is planned but not shipped — Postgres is required today.

No payment facilitator

BeepBite records tenders; it never touches your money. "Card" means your own card machine on your own counter. No PCI scope, no settlement delay, no cut of your revenue.

Every integration is opt-in

WhatsApp, maps and AI are each off unless you supply your own credentials. As Discord, Slack and email adapters land, the same rule applies — they activate only when you turn them on.

works today · Postgres required
# 1. Database
createdb beepbite

# 2. Configure — set DATABASE_URL and JWT_SECRET
cp .env.example .env

# 3. Migrate
cd backend && go run ./cmd/migrate --env=local --up

# 4. API
go run ./cmd/server --env=local

# 5. App
cd .. && npm install && npm run dev        # http://localhost:5173

# want something to look at first — full demo restaurant
cd backend && go run ./cmd/seedcopper --env=local --clean

Part of VulOS

Vulos = free, open-source software + two paid services. The Vulos OS, all its apps (BeepBite included) and the app store are OSS and free — you self-host them. You self-provision and self-pay your own box; Vulos does not host or provision boxes. Vulos bills for only two things: Relay (reachability) and backup storage (buckets). There is no compute billing, no mail billing, and no app-store subscription.

  • Vulos OS — the web-native desktop shell that hosts the apps
  • Vulos Office — docs, sheets, slides, PDF and whiteboards
  • Vulos Files — file storage and P2P sharing, built into the OS
  • Vulos Relay — sovereign reachability fabric — one of the two paid services
  • llmux — sovereign AI gateway

PIM is bring-your-own (Mail / Calendar / Contacts); chat and video are third-party (Matrix/Element, Element Call / Jitsi) — not Vulos products.

BeepBite's role: the restaurant point-of-sale and ordering app. It runs standalone against your own Postgres, and is designed to also be hosted as an app by the Vulos OS — the same binary, with the OS wiring identity and scoped storage in front of it. Relay, the control plane and DMTAP are optional seams; a hard runtime dependency on any of them is forbidden.
